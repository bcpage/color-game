Paradox: Banach-Tarski

Given a solid sphere in ℝ³, it is possible to decompose it into a finite number of disjoint subsets, which can then be put back together in a different way to yield two identical copies of the original sphere.

5 pieces
reassemble (rotate + translate)
2 spheres, same size

Proved by Stefan Banach and Alfred Tarski in 1924. The theorem is a consequence of the Axiom of Choice — a statement about selecting elements from infinite collections that most mathematicians accept but which leads, here, to this conclusion.

The decomposition is not physically realizable. The five pieces are not solid objects in any intuitive sense. They are non-measurable sets: collections of points so irregular that they have no well-defined volume. They cannot be constructed, only proved to exist.

No material is created. No conservation law is violated. The theorem operates entirely within the mathematics of infinite point sets, where the ordinary rules about volume and measure do not apply.

The Banach-Tarski theorem is sometimes called a paradox, but it is not a contradiction — it is a theorem. It is true. The discomfort it produces is a property of human intuition, not of mathematics.

The only way to escape the conclusion is to reject the Axiom of Choice. Some mathematicians do.
