Paradox: Grandfather

You travel back in time and prevent your grandfather from meeting your grandmother. Your parents are never born. You are never born. You cannot travel back in time. Your grandfather meets your grandmother. Your parents are born. You are born. You travel back in time and prevent your grandfather from meeting your grandmother.

The grandfather paradox was introduced by science-fiction writer René Barjavel in 1943. It remains the canonical argument against unrestricted time travel — not because it proves time travel impossible, but because it demonstrates that unrestricted time travel produces logical contradiction.

Proposed resolutions include: the Novikov self-consistency principle (only self-consistent timelines can occur — you will always fail to prevent your grandfather from meeting your grandmother); many-worlds branching (you create a new branch and your action prevents a version of yourself, not the version that sent you back); and temporal immunity (you exist outside the timeline you modify).

None of these resolutions are provable. All are logically consistent. The paradox does not resolve itself.
